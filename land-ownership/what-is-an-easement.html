It can also be created by the legal doctrine of prescriptive easement, which is an important aspect of understanding what is an easement. This type of easement is based on the law of adverse possession and arises when a non-owner of property continuously uses another person’s land for a specified purpose for a statutory period. Types of Easements in New Jersey

An easement is a non-possessory property right giving the holder the right to use a portion of someone else’s land for specific purposes, which is why understanding what is an easement is essential in New Jersey. The most common type of easement in New Jersey involves allowing neighbors to drive across each other’s land for access to a road. Utility easements that grant service providers access to install and maintain water, sewer, cable, telephone, power, gas, and other services are also very common.

Easements are commonly found in real estate transactions and are generally created either by express or implied conveyance. Those that are expressly created must comply with the Statute of Frauds and be recorded in order to be effective. Those that are created by prescriptive easement, on the other hand, require a long history of notorious use and can only be terminated by a recorded deed or court action. Understanding what is an easement and its creation process is vital for property owners and buyers.

The person that holds an easement is called the “adverse owner” and the land benefited by the easement is called the “dominant estate.” Easements can be affirmative or negative, although most are affirmative. Negative easements are less common and usually involve preserving a person’s view or access to light by restricting what can be done on neighboring property. Knowing what is an easement and its types helps in identifying the rights associated with a property.

There are many different types of easements that can be created, but in general, an easement is enforceable only when it is formally transferred or created and then recorded in the land records. The recordation process requires a written document, the proper signatures (in most states this includes a notary), and is then recorded at the county clerk or register of deeds where the land is located. This process highlights the importance of understanding what is an easement in legal terms.

Once an easement is recorded, it becomes an enforceable right that will remain in effect even if the dominant or servient estate is sold to another person or entity. This is a fundamental aspect of what makes an easement so valuable to those who hold them and why they are often a significant component of the value of property.
