Understanding Special Needs Trusts in New Jersey

You can protect your eligibility for government benefits like SSI and Medicaid by placing assets in a Special Needs Trust. These kinds of trusts are sometimes referred to as (d)(4)(A) or 1396p(d)(4)(A) trusts because they are named for the section of federal law that authorizes them. The trustee manages the funds, and the money in these trusts does not count toward your public benefits' resource limits. However, the person who sets up the trust must do it correctly, often requiring the experience of special needs lawyers near me, or else it could affect your public benefits. You must also be careful about how you spend the money. If you get the trustee to pay a provider directly, the amount paid must be documented. Payments that you take directly can be considered income and may cause you to lose your benefits.

There are three common types of Special Needs Trusts. The right one for you depends on your circumstances. First Party Special Needs Trusts (also called Medicaid Payback Trusts) are used when you have accumulated assets, inherited money, or received a court settlement and actually own the money yourself. It used to be illegal for a person with a disability to set up their own First Party Special Needs Trust, so a parent, grandparent, guardian, or the court, often with the help of special needs lawyers near me, had to do it for them.

In a Pooled Special Needs Trust, money is collected from many people and put into an investment account. This type of trust is often set up by a nonprofit organization, which takes care of tax preparation, invests the money, and acts as trustee. After you die, the assets in this trust must be used to repay the state for Medicaid expenses that it paid on your behalf. Special needs lawyers near me can help you determine if a Pooled Special Needs Trust is the best option for your situation and ensure it is set up correctly.

Consulting with special needs lawyers near me can provide peace of mind and ensure that the trust complies with all legal requirements. These special needs lawyers near me can assist with the documentation and management of the trust, ensuring that it does not jeopardize your eligibility for government benefits.

Navigating Special Needs Trusts With New Jersey Attorneys

If your loved one has a disability, you may be concerned about how to provide care without jeopardizing their access to needs-based government programs. These include Medicaid health care coverage and monthly disability checks from Supplemental Security Income (SSI). Special needs lawyers near me can help establish a special needs trust (SNT) as a way to protect assets so that contributions made by family members and other loved ones won’t affect the beneficiary’s eligibility for these programs, which have asset and income limits.

The trustee of a special needs trust can be either an individual or a legal entity. A trustee manages the assets in the trust, and a written agreement (trust instrument) explains the trustee’s duties and responsibilities. Like any other legal arrangement, special needs trusts have specific terms that vary from state to state. The language can be difficult to understand and often requires special needs lawyers near me to ensure the document meets all the requirements of the beneficiary, the trustee, and any other parties involved. Special needs lawyers near me can provide the necessary experience to navigate these complexities and ensure compliance with state laws.

SNTs are commonly used to pay for supplemental items that will improve the beneficiary’s quality of life, such as therapies, recreation, and vacations. They can also be used to cover expenses that are not covered by government assistance, including home care aides and transportation costs. The trustee of the trust will usually only use funds from the SNT to help supplement government benefits, and the trustee must regularly report to Social Security and/or the state Medicaid agency on the trust’s activity. Special needs lawyers near me can assist the trustee in understanding and fulfilling these reporting requirements.

The type of SNT you choose depends on the situation, but there are two main types: first-party and third-party. A first-party SNT, which is sometimes called a self-settled trust or (d)(4)(A) trust, is funded with assets that belong to the beneficiary. These may include gifts, inheritances from parents or other relatives, and proceeds from life insurance policies. A first-party SNT must include a Medicaid repayment provision, which stipulates that any remaining assets will be used to reimburse Medicaid after the beneficiary’s death or termination of the trust, whichever occurs later. Special needs lawyers near me can help draft these provisions to ensure they comply with federal and state regulations.

A third-party SNT is a pooled trust administered by a nonprofit organization. These funds are contributed by many families and other donors to benefit multiple beneficiaries. The trustee of the pooled trust is chosen by the nonprofit organization, and the trustee will act on behalf of all beneficiaries in the fund. Special needs lawyers near me can offer guidance on selecting the appropriate nonprofit organization and trustee to manage the pooled trust effectively.
